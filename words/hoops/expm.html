Lessons From a Failed Stat

  • May 13th, 2021

  • 8 min read

In the spring of 2019, I tried to build an all-in-one aggregate NBA statistic. At the time I was a busy computer science undergrad with a penchant for procrastinating on the important things, and marrying my love for basketball with my growing skillset as a mathematician and programmer felt natural. In particular, I was drawn to the idea by Jacob Goldstein, current research analyst for the Washington Wizards, Mystics, and Go-Go, who built the then-essential, now-proprietary PIPM statistic when he was an undergrad himself. It seemed like a reasonable endeavor and I felt prepared enough to try for myself.

My vision was grand and naive. As a follower of the ever-miserable Chicago Bulls, I was frustrated by the most common source of confusion that stemmed from use of plus-minus derived aggregate stats like PIPM: the results. Low usage, high efficiency players from winning teams tend to be rewarded over high usage, medium efficiency players from losing teams, and why shouldn’t they, if they have a larger part of a team’s success? Isn’t winning why they play the game at all? It’s a reasonable conclusion, but it can misinform. For me, this came to a head when an elderly Bulls fan went on a Twitter rant claiming that he’d rather max Jeremy Lamb than Zach LaVine - ESPN’s RPM (whose formula has since been revised) cited as the chief evidence.

The argument can be made that we live in a world severed from widespread statistical comprehension. Mathematical communication has always been a problem for humans, but perhaps most famously we don’t find probabilities intuitive at all. The foundation of confusion in statistics lies in the strange, but accurate insights we are able to extract from computation, like why it’s always beneficial to switch prizes on the Monty Hall Show, or why it’s more likely than not that 2 people in a room of 23 share a birthday, despite 366 possible dates. For some reason, the human brain doesn't expect these outcomes.

Despite a gradual uptick in the comprehension of statistics in the broader NBA community, professional basketball is not an exception to the confusion; on the contrary, sports are possibly the most common arena for misunderstaning math, I imagine because it draws in people from a variety of backgrounds, not all technical. If you tell the average NBA fan that Dunks & Threes’ all-in-one stat EPM ranks Jakob Poeltl as a better defender than Bam Adebayo, they might find it silly, or worse, use the existence of that fact to dismiss the site’s efforts entirely. The dissonance, however impacted by external factors like media coverage and marketing efforts it might be, creates a cultural divide between “the numbers” and “the game.”

But what if those fans are right to call out the discrepancies? To me, there was nothing appreciably wrong with the top-down plus-minus approach, but if it didn’t match a de facto reality, shouldn’t it be held up to scrutiny? This was the question burning a hole in my pocket, the one I wanted to try to solve with my project.

After some soul-searching and brainstorming about the math, I created a rough draft of what I called Experimental Plus-Minus (EXPM) - its key feature being a complete dismissal of plus-minus data, instead building the skillset of each player up from scratch. Similar methodology was adopted in the days where the box score was the only thing widely available, and metrics like PER, Win Shares, and Box Plus-Minus (BPM) were born. But in 2019, there was so much more to offer - extensive lineup data, robust defensive tracking, rough playtype frequencies, and so on. My plan was to take advantage of that newer, deeper NBA data to build a “smarter” version of those original box-score statistics.

(As you might have guessed, EXPM is a misnomer - the "PM" part of it makes no sense when plus-minus data isn't used. I genuinely just thought it would be funny if the statistic had a similar name to EXP, the basic character-leveling currency used in thousands of role-playing video games.)

EXPM was built to be comprised of multiple sub-statistics that calculated a player’s value in a particular useful area, which would then sum to the total. Not all areas were actually given representation, only a very Moreyball-charged subset that I, at the time, considered to be important to the sport: three-point shooting, passing, scoring at the rim, self-creation, and a clean-up component for offensive rebounds and screen assists. Everything else was completely omitted from the calculation, the theory being that in a modern game, only modern skills should be rewarded.

In retrospect, my attempt is an unabashed failure at actually representing basketball value. My attempt at punishing inefficient players failed, as the bad shots were never considered in the formulation and thus couldn't hurt them. I never built the daily scraper to update the box score sources after new games had completed, instead working with outdated numbers until the regular season ended and there was no more need to pull them. Did I mention that I also never got around to the defensive half of this stat? Sometimes it just isn’t meant to be.

I can’t even really dissect the mathematics of EXPM here, as most of it is beyond salvaging. Still, there are a few takeaways from EXPM that I think can help shed some insight on basketball numbers and what that information actually tells you.

Ultimately, EXPM’s fatal flaw was in the premise. Every time you build upwards from existing stats, you’re trapped by an unreliable present. While we might freely tease the first generation of aggregate stats today for their simplicity, their creators couldn’t yet factor in the wealth of information we’re able to access today because it just didn’t exist. They were advanced and all-encompassing for their time, but times change. Similarly, the “advanced box score” as we call it today might look paltry in another 30 years as automatic video trackers get more sophisticated, specifically on the defensive end. The platonic ideal of an all-in-one statistic is "good plays minus bad plays," and that reality might be soon.

It’s also healthy to recognize that these numbers in the box score are just a low-resolution interpretation of a game that’s played in real life. The physics of the ball and the players colliding and bouncing alone is immensely complicated, with the difference between, say, a made pass for a 3 and a deflected pass hit out of bounds coming down to a matter of tiny micro angles of the ball leaving a player’s hands. Nearly infinite layers of complexity and nuance - defensive schemes, individual matchups and mismatches, set designs, physicality and positioning, footwork and speed changes, recent-possession momentum, nerves, confidence - go unquantified and unaccounted for in even the most advanced statistical suites available.

When confronted by a game with innumerable missing data points, plus-minus data emerges as an incredibly elegant and brilliant solution. Working backwards from the results, factoring in who was on the court, during which minutes, against what competition, does capture quite a lot about the happenings of that game. If fifty factors influence the outcome of a basketball game, the on-off data will invariably capture them, or at least their “shadow” - manifesting as runs, stops, and streaks. This is still true if there are a hundred factors, or a thousand, or ten-thousand. While we can’t glean every insight from plus-minus data, it is still descriptive - and maybe comprehensive - in its own abstract, silhouetted way.

In contrast, bottom-up approaches must always be lossy, shedding holistic value as it incorporates more individual subfactors that may not paint the full picture. Worse still is that this can spark unproductive, unsolvable discussions about the relative value of basketball skills. If you build a statistic from the box score, by definition your statistic must be assembled using some formulation of that existing numeric data. Doing so can prompt debates that cannot be conclusively determined, like “how much should a point count versus an assist, or a rebound?” This ultimately makes the result of the statistic only as reliable and predictive as the basketball philosophy of the statistician, which may not even match reality at all.

EXPM is absolutely littered with these decisions, because the advanced box score blows the door wide open on what can be considered a productive basketball event. How much should a passing assist be worth versus a screen assist? Both create a field goal attempt, but now you have to quantify the risk of a moving screen violation versus that of a passing turnover. How about the value of a contested rebound versus an uncontested one? Do you use raw assists or do you approximate "expected" assists by multiplying potential assists by the league average efficiency? Ultimately, these decisions vary from person to person, alterating (and obfuscating) the impact they aim to quantify.

There is also a significant problem of “invisible” value. The plus-minus approach rightfully envisions a basketball games as a series of possessions with score consequences, but the box score does not abide by those rules. A good example is a possession wherein a player drives to the rim, kicking the ball out to a semi-open corner shooter who in turn swings it to a more open elbow shooter, who makes the shot. This happens all the time, whether it's Mike Conley to Joe Ingles to Bojan Bogdanovic, or Kyle Lowry to OG Anunoby to Gary Trent Jr.

With the plus-minus approach, all three players (and their two other teammates) are rewarded for that successful play. From the box-score, unless hockey assists are implemented, the player that kicked off the sequence with the drive-and-kick goes completely unrewarded, instead resulting in an assist for the corner shooter and a made field goal for the elbow shooter. The two weak-side teammates whose non-presence made the sequence possible (maybe crucially stretching the floor to give a lane for the initial drive) get no credit at all. It’s a headache.

Finally, EXPM fails because of intentional value overlap, where a single possession can positively or negatively impact a player more than once. Three separate sub-categories of EXPM - three-point attempts, at-rim attempts, and self-created attempts - can easily overlap with one another in two obvious and common examples: the self-created drive to the basket and the pull-up three pointer. These are incredibly valuable shots for a player to produce, arguably the most important in the sport right now, and because of that I didn’t mind that they were double-counted - I figured it was a reasonable reward. Still, “double” is a flippant, unscientific multiplier that isn’t really based in anything meaningful besides it being convenient for my calculation. Not only is it not elegant, but it's incomprehensible.

There are still good uses for the box score. To determine who is the best at any individual skill, it's nearly impossible to take any other approach, and without it as a supplement to the aggregate stats, we lose a lot of definition. It obviously isn't tenable for the basketball community at large to talk exclusively in good/bad binaries when the beauty of the game lies in distinct playstyles, how they interact and clash, how a player might exceed or fall short of the sum of their parts.

That said, it might really be time to hang up it up as formulation for any aggregate ranking. Even with the significantly more robust tracking in the modern day, the lingering issue of relative skill value is too large of an obstacle to reasonably surmise. Perhaps one day we might be able to mine plus-minus data and extract the exact value of every individually tracked box score stat, but today is not that day. Even if an answer is reached, it might not be consistent from year to year or even game to game, rendering it too unstable for widespread casual usage.

I can't totally rule it out, maybe somebody out there that's both much smarter than I am and has more creative ways to leverage the ever-growing repository of NBA tracking data will crack the code. But one thing is for sure, there is no need for an EXPM or anything like it.
