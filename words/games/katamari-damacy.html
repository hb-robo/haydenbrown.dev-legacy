A Cosmic Power Fantasy

  • August 23, 2020

  • 15 min read

Released in 2004 for the PlayStation 2, Katamari Damacy has become famous for a few things: a brilliant soundtrack popular even with those who haven't played it; a colorful, surrealist art direction; and a core concept both intuitive and bizarre. Katamari roughly translates to “clump” in English, and as you might expect, the game is about rolling your clump around, collecting everything you can see until it’s sufficiently large.

What elevates this simple idea is not only the context of humans as the playerbase, who are intimately familiar with the objects and structures that can be collected, but also a presentation that delights in the chaos you unfold. The player is treated to a whirlwind of jazzy bubblegum electronica, bright pastel colors, and unfettered, wanton destruction - it’s a complete sensory overload, and one that is fueled almost entirely by play, making it impossible to replicate in any other medium.

The player’s role as the Prince is a daunting one: you must rebuild all of the stars in the night sky after your father, the ostentatious King of the Cosmos, destroys them on a whim. True to the fragility of the omnipotent, the King laments the stars’ absence in the just after destroying the last one, and tasks you with collecting enough stuff from Earth to use as material to rebuild the cosmos. Your trusty katamari is used to roll up objects, people, and creatures of all sorts, indiscriminately taking raw matter regardless of its intended purpose or sentience.

The catch is that the King of the Cosmos is an allegedly busy guy and he only gives you a handful of minutes to create a ball of a given diameter, making where to go and what to pick up a series of continuous, frantic mental decisions for the entirety of the stage. You control your ball of stuff with the two analog sticks, each vaguely representing the Prince’s hands, and barrel through the environment before you in the time allotted.

The sense of scale and perspective was the first thing that grabs you. The tutorial level takes place atop a kotatsu, the player rolling up candies and matchsticks and dominoes as the size of one themselves. As the levels go by, you either start from or are given more time to reach a larger diameter for your katamari, and this naturally changes the bounds of the area - eventually tackling the rest of the house, the yard, the local neighborhood, the nearby cities and finally entire islands in the sea.

This scale is emphasized in gameplay with an extremely literal mass hierarchy. For each of the game’s myriad of things, there is a hidden minimum diameter needed to attach it to your katamari - any attempt to accumulate it before reaching that threshold will aggressively bounce you away. Crucially, nothing in the entire game is exempt from this rule - out of the hundreds of thousands of objects in this game, they either are yours to take now or later, it’s just a matter of becoming large enough to do so.

This gives way to some creative level structures, where areas in a stage can be "zoned off" until later simply by containing too many of an item you can't pick up yet, giving a semi-intended route though these otherwise unbounded playgrounds. A personal favorite of mine is an alleyway with bowling pins, which both stands as an initially imposing phalanx for the player’s progress in the area, and also perfectly congratulates the player in a fun way the instant they are able to be bowled through.

It’s easy to see yourself as something of a nuisance in the early stages, running amok in some poor soul’s house, stealing their waste and belongings. There are much larger structures you must abide by as the bounds of the stage, and any living creature poses a threat to you, smacking you away and knocking some clumped objects off of your katamari in the process. However, this concept comes full circle in an entirely logical and empowering way.

A fairly universal experience with the game that I’ve noticed is that everyone remembers their first encounter with a cat. They are the most aggressive obstacle early in the game, running up to you if they see you - and why wouldn’t they when your clump looks like a toy tailor-made for them? It smacks you around a little until it gets bored and walks off, as cats do. Starting from stage 3, however, there is enough stuff and a long enough time limit on the stage to roll around this large loop and come back to the cat large enough to cause it to start running away from you, turning the previous dynamic of the encounter on its head - the cat has become a plaything for you, fodder for your mission.

In the later levels, the sense of destruction you're causing can become intoxicating. Similar role reversals to the cat occur throughout the game with human beings in a small outdoor marketplace, cars on a loop of highway, and finally ships and military vessels that choose to blast away at the incomprehensible clump of stuff put before them - a funny, depressing, terminally human conclusion. Overcoming your obstacles through sheer heft feels good in a primal sort of way, as you stage a literal conquest of the environment before you. Soon enough, you'll pluck islands from the sea like you're waxing hair off of your body.

From a gameplay perspective, the most difficult thing to do at any given time is to simply get a reign on this massive thing you’re pushing about. The katamari is controlled with the analog sticks exclusively - if both sticks point in any direction the ball will roll that way, if they are opposite of one another, the camera rotates either clockwise or counter-clockwise to create a new trajectory. This in and of itself does not pose any special consideration from the player - if the game had simply visually applied the objects collected while maintaining a perfectly spherical collision area, there wouldn’t be much challenge, it’d be like setting a quickly growing Super Monkey Ball character loose in the game world - perfect responsiveness from every angle and in every direction.

Katamari Damacy upends this by physically sticking the model to your katamari in a way that the object’s previous collision data is now an extension of yours in the exact way it was initially touched. This has some interesting consequences - one comes early on in the form of any number of utility poles and streetlamps as they stick to your katamari in an asymmetric way, becoming an impedance to your movement. However, because the katamari’s radial velocity is preserved, this also has the useful side effect of being able to leverage that new arm as a sort of pole vault, reaching higher locations. This is never once required of the player, but its consequences as both a hindrance and advantage are completely emergent, the outcome dependent on the way in which the player collects the item in question.

There are other interesting examples as well. Rolling atop a row of aligned, uniformly spaced items - a shelf of books, for instance - applies them radially around the katamari, giving it a shape more akin to a wheel or disc. Like with the poles from before, you may try to ignore this constraint and wobble around, eventually filling the shallower sides, but you could also lean into the temporary advantages of your new state and barrel through the level like a loose tire. Again, this is completely emergent - some items are set up in a way to encourage these strange shapes, but the direction the player hits them from can’t be guaranteed, and so each instance of it is quasi-random and driven entirely by the player. This simple moment-to-moment difference in control can make repeated runs through the same level feel very different from one another.

Mercifully, Katamari Damacy does not fall into the pitfalls of stuffy 3D platforming completionism that could have plagued the game if helmed by another development team. The natural step forward in the gamification of a katamari is to go from collect “enough” to collect everything, but this is never required of you, and in fact I think it’s deliberately avoided as much as possible. In addition to the time constraint - which is tight enough to encourage you to leave areas with items that are too small to meaningfully increase your katamari’s size - there is also a sort of mass tier system, where reaching certain katamari size thresholds will pan the camera out and de-load the smaller objects while loading in far-off areas that are now accessible with a little more effort.

This has the unfortunate side effect of transforming a certain inner radius of your katamari into a generic sphere again, keeping the most recently collected items intact, which somewhat dulls the emergent movement discusses early. However, it’s pretty unreasonable for the game to have to keep track of the exact collision of the thousands of miniscule objects you’ve accumulated, and in its defense these load sequences only happen a handful of times even on the longest levels. This is a necessary constraint in order to achieve the spectacle of the gameplay itself - you can’t grow your clump from cat toy to the size of a small building without acknowledging that some items are simply too insignificant for the console to care about anymore.

In an exaggerated example, the marketplace where you spend most of Level 5 is connected to the house you begin the game in by a steep road. If you gain enough size and have enough time remaining in the stage, you can roll back up the road and pluck the entire house right out of the ground as a single object. The kotatsu you start the game atop of is no longer large enough to even be perceived as something picked up. Outside of being incredibly empowering to the player, this dynamic scale and time limit naturally disincentivizes a completionist approach to the game because collecting everything requires a playstyle that slows the katamari’s growth as much as possible, which is in direct opposition to the win condition.

I think a way to articulate the elegance of these constraints is that they paradoxically wrap around again in the form of freedom - you are not bound by the natural impulses and habits that games teach you, and instead you are free in the most literal sense to move as quickly as possible and be as sloppy as you like, every item and creature vying for your destructive attention. There are no style points here, and the game actively rejects any methodological approach by the player, instead reveling in the mess.

The sound design in Katamari Damacy not only enhances this feeling of freedom but elevates it to outright catharsis. There is a plucky little sound effect whenever you pick something up that randomly deviates in pitch. Approaching a large number of objects causes the sound to play in a satisfying burst, and it gets better the larger you grow. If your katamari is sufficiently large to just touch both sides of a busy street, you may hear this sound hundreds of times as you roll down the way, making you feel like you’re making progress in your rate of accumulation - a comically literal instance of the snowball effect.

The presence or absence of the little “chunk” noise can also help you indicate whether the area you’re in is a smart one to be in, as the sparseness or density of useful objects that can be picked up can be felt just by rolling around and listening for the sound. Entering an area initially rich with items to pick up and cleaning it out until there’s hardly anything left to grab also gives the impression that you’ve sort of tidied up the place, like when a vacuum cleaner stops rattling.

Katamari Damacy’s universally acclaimed soundtrack also strongly benefits the overall experience, setting a fantastical tone and - bewilderingly - doing right by the game’s core themes in its design. It draws from a suitably vast set of influences by incorporating a large number of musicians who each made their own tracks, as if trying to match the katamari you build. Instead of feeling patchwork, though, it has a cohesive quality thanks to its playful nature and emphasis on uptempo rhythms, giving a pace to the player’s actions.

The musical direction primarily draws from shibuya-kei, a style of Japanese pop from the 90s: it’s something like a modern, jazzier take on traditional pop music, incorporating both electronic and orchestral elements in equal part to feel fresh and futuristic. Its emphasis on fusion and creating something new from pieces of old is quite fitting for a game about turning manmade objects into stars. Katamari isn’t wholly derivative, though, and in some regards it was actually quite hip at the time.

You can find bits and pieces of early 2000s lounge revival, the emerging Japanese glitch pop scene, samba and flamenco, tech house, IDM, vocal jazz, Michael Bolton, j-rap, and what I have come to call “miniature electronics” a la Daedelus or Shlohmo (let me know if there is a real name for this). All this is to say that Katamari Damacy isn’t just well composed and interesting for its time, it was also ahead of or up to date with musical trends at its release.

The varied score might seem at odds with the gameplay premise, which is so perfectly singular and consistent, but in a stroke of utter brilliance, the soundtrack itself is not meant to color the moment-to-moment gameplay (although it often does) - it is also an exploration of how musical responses to the katamari would play out in real life. The vocal tracks are often self-referential, flamboyantly posing the music as a creation of the alternative polygonal Earth you play in.

Ballads about both the katamari and the one rolling it gives the impression that the player, through their actions with their clump of chaos, has become the ultimate muse for artists everywhere. Interestingly, their work never strays into nihilism, instead tenderly exploring the event as a frame or metaphor for their own emotions and desires. This could be cynically dismissed as a branding effort, like when the Tellytubbies sing their own names, but the effort put into the arrangements and the way the lyrics explore the katamari’s place in the world is simply too well-considered for me to leave it at that.

Here is just a smattering of the lyrical content: a semi-conscious rap about getting caught up in the power of the Katamari and regretting the destruction caused; a wistful longing for the person rolling the katamari, arguably the player; the fatalist romanticization of two lovers being pulled into the katamari together; the exploration of being turned into a star by the katamari as wish fulfillment; the sexualization of being consumed by a katamari [NOT A JOKE]; a nostalgic memory of following the katamari around as a child as a metaphor for adolesencent naivety; and finally, heartfelt optimism that the katamari is the great equalizer, something that finally brings humanity together and teaches them to love one another in their brief final moments before becoming a star in the sky.

I strongly doubt there will ever be this deep of a musical exploration of a game’s concept again. The songs actually being catchy, well-produced, and fairly unique in gaming on top of it all sells this completely - and this point’s relative obscurity is an unfortunate consequence of the language barrier for the West. I have never heard music for any game that manages to sound as fitting in game, as interesting out of game, and create its own striking identity as well as Katamari Damacy’s, and as far as I’m concerned this is among the best original soundtracks ever made in any medium.

Series creator Keita Takahashi has spoken about Katamari as a critique of consumerist culture, and I think that lens is the best way to understand the game's vision. The number of different objects that can be rolled up is staggering, and even through hours of gameplay there will certainly be some that you haven't seen yet. The Collection page lets you take a look at everything you’ve ever rolled up, and while many players might look through it fondly, remembering their adventures as the Prince, I can’t help but think about how much work it must’ve been to make all of models. It seems the hardest part of making a critique of consumerism is properly recreating the staggering amount of things the earth has.

The result of this fittingly quantity-focused approach is a surrealist, brightly-colored art direction that is what I’d call tastefully low-poly. The pastels in the palette work nicely with the soft blur from the SD output of the PS2, giving the visuals a warm quality, and the choice for a stylized simplification over realism surely made the task of modeling all of these things less daunting for the art team.

At the end of each stage, The King of the Cosmos lambasts or praises you based on your collection efforts, but will always end his monologue with the same line: "My, Earth really is full of things." It is up to interpretation if this is meant to represent some divine condescension of mankind's relentless hoarding, or a critique of the actual utility of most things in their current state outside of kindling for a boulder of stuff, or a more abstract desire of the creator to channel his distaste for the manufacturing of useless trinkets that comprise a majority of our landfills, to literally shoot all of this garbage into space, where it may actually be of use for something, and to clean our planet of their presence.

The fact that the people who helped create all of these things are commonly caught in the mayhem serves only to amplify the stance, whether you see those acts of abduction to be punishment for mankind or an act no different than picking up anything else. Perhaps it's even justifiable as an alleviation of a burden on the Earth itself.

On a smaller narrative scale, you learn of a human perspective on the events taking place through small cutscenes of the Hoshino family, who witness the Katamari as they travel across the country to see the astronaut father launch his rocket to the moon. The kids see news stories about stars disappearing and reappearing, and even catch glimpses of the King himself, but their mother always just misses these scenes and refuses to believe such ridiculous stories. Inevitably, the adults are forced to accept the situation when the father's launch is canceled after the moon can no longer be found in the night sky - a reconstruction project that you as the Prince just hadn't gotten to yet.

This second story gives an interesting perspective of childlike wonder on your anti-consumerist crusade, re-contextualizing the game as what it appears to be at first glance - a fantastical mythos of childish imagination. Katamari Damacy is both the work of a discontent adult, disgusted with the planet around him, and the boy he once was, wide-eyed for stories of the otherworldly.

Some additional perspective on the events of the game comes from the player - despite being an alien, totally separated from the day-to-day life of Earth, the Prince is of course meant to be controlled by a human. The contrast between how the Prince and King feel about the surplus of useless stuff littered about the face of the Earth and how the player feels as they do the collecting is in itself commentary.

The dissonance, if there is any, questions the utility of the things the player will see everywhere they go, every day. Perhaps your katamari takes away something meaningful to your real life, like an artbook or a game console. Perhaps the player is a businessman by trade, and in the game he abducts dozens of suited salarymen, maybe even an entire office. The point isn’t necessarily to discard what you care about or who you are, but to offer a cosmic perspective on their relative insignificance. Katamari Damacy isn’t just an absurd game, it’s an absurd-IST one, recognizing that the world is chaotic and arbitrary.

Seeing a city picked clean off the ground makes one wonder about how the land of their own neighborhood might look if a big space clump took it away instead. Maybe this sounds like pointless nonsense to you, but it is significant as a form of therapy. Many people, our neighbors and family included, struggle with the pointless cruelty of the world we inhabit. Billions of people alive today will live their entire lives fighting through it. Although the game doesn’t offer an alternative to the human condition, it does inspire wonder about how different it could all be, and how things came to be this way.

Katamari Damacy achieves the complete actualization of its concept in so many regards it feels impossible that it was created by such a species as flawed as humans - especially if you consider the apathetic attitude the game has towards mankind’s material obsessions. At the same time, it is possibly the most beautifully human game ever made, a thorough exploration of all things joyful, wondrous, and freeing. For a game as superficially destructive as this, that element is something enormously difficult to retain.

Katamari Damacy manages to fulfill the player’s desire to bring chaos to order and to make order from chaos simultaneously. These parallel concepts result in something that feels close to the Law of Conservation of Matter - we cannot create matter from nothing, nor destroy it to the point of nonexistence. We can only change its form, repurposing it for some cosmic ideal. You don’t have to dig too far to figure out what else is being conserved in this process, either. Katamari might mean “clump,” but Damacy means “soul,” and that’s exactly what the game has in spades.
