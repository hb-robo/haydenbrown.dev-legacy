100 Word Reviews: 2021

  • Various, 2021

  • 5 min read

This is a collection of all of the smaller thoughts I had about the games I played in 2021, good and bad. If a game doesn't inspire enough in me to write a whole essay on it, I whittle it down to its essence and throw it in here. New write-ups will be added periodically throughout the year.

Hyper Light Drifter (2016) - ★★★☆

dev. Heart Machine, dir. Alx Preston

Heart Machine’s debut makes a meaningful discovery: that imagery alone can partially substitute conventional storytelling.

What is discarded in detail is kept afloat by evocative symbolism and context clues, leaving a mostly intact narrative experience unshackled by the burdens of shallow, explanatory lore. The only real consequence is a snappier pace that complements its zippy combat, an evolution of Zelda DNA with twin-stick shooter elements, a trendy dash button, and highly curated, DOOM-esque combat rooms.

In lieu of instructions, the player pays closer attention to the spaces before them, organically digesting every secret, every battle, every wordless scene of spectacle.

January 2021, PC

Valkyria Chronicles (2008) - ★★½☆

dev. SEGA, dir. Shuntaro Tanaka

SEGA’s hammy, pseudo-WW2 tactics romp is about the draft and its consequences, of ill-suited soldiers dreaming of their old lives. Your squadron is filled with imperfect people, their allergies, phobias, orientations, and prejudices producing strengths and weaknesses in their new roles.

On the battlefield these myriad perspectives are visited personally, the player forced to inhabit every body and fire every shot themselves, the controlled character’s traits manifesting as statistical impacts that inform (and even disturb!) your decision-making process.

It inspires empathy, but unfortunately misses the logical conclusion of that inspiration: a similarly flawed and human army on the other side.

January 2021, PC

Kirby Super Star Ultra (2008) - ★★½☆

dev. HAL Laboratory, dir. Shinya Kumazaki

Some developers of Super Star Ultra were disappointed with the game’s final title. They felt they’d done enough work and added enough content to completely separate their work from the original Super Star for SNES.

Unfortunately, all this amounts to legendary hubris - Ultra is a tit-for-tat remake, not only reworking the same Kirby spritework and copy mechanics in circulation since 2002 to match the source material, but totally and faithfully replicating Super Star ’s concept and structure.

The new content here simply tweaks the classic game modes, extending the runtime but certainly not transforming the experience. A fitting title after all.

February 2021, NDS

DOOM (2016) - ★★★☆

dev. Id Software, dir. Hugo Martin

1994’s DOOM bore a thousand children, but few with family resemblance. 2016's DOOM suffers from this, manifesting as a glossy, Halo ’d-up take on its namesake with fanservice garnish, but still manages to successfully modernize an older paradigm of shooter combat by recreating the pace, intensity and micromanagement.

Each enemy type disrupts the player’s movement differently, and ammunition is scarce unless you finish off staggered demons with melee attacks. In concert, the player’s eyes and ears fire on all cylinders, picking priority targets and finding creative ways to leverage the densely arranged arenas. It just needs to stick to satanic horror.

February 2021, PC

Kirby: Squeak Squad (2006) - ★½☆☆

dev. HAL Laboratory, dir. Takashi Hamamura

Kirby 's first mainline post-Sakurai platformer is, much like the treasure it features, more interesting before you open it up. Below-par level design, an exceedingly short runtime, and recycled content aside, the few fresh ideas - chests of fanservice dotting the levels like Amazing Mirror , thieving mice enemies to race and battle, and a strikingly clunky inventory system for Kirby’s stomach - only mar the core gameplay’s pace and charms.

Squeak Squad managing to fail without venturing beyond the shadows of its predecessors is not insignificant: it makes a franchise rooted in consistency feel shallow, sowing doubt in Kirby 's merits to begin with.

March 2021, NDS

Kingdom Hearts: Melody of Memory (2020) - ★★½☆

dev. Square Enix & Indieszero, dir. Tetsuya Nomura & Masanobu Suzui

Kingdom Hearts owes everything to Yoko Shimomura. Nineteen years later, it finally erects a playable monument to her brilliant, rambunctious compositions.

Melody of Memory not only sheds the mechanical baggage of its Theatrhythm lineage, but commits to its own rhythmic pleasures by preserving the physicality of the mainline entries’ combat system, ramping up the player’s melodic responsibility throughout each track.

Unsurprisingly, the experience degrades as it travels along the series timeline, Shimomura pushed aside for cinematic scenes that tie into the recent entries. This is the nature of compilations, but it’s hard to watch the series die a second time.

March 2021, NDS

Luigi's Mansion 3 (2019) - ★★½☆

dev. Next Level Games & Nintendo EAD, dir. Bryce Holliday

Having missed Dark Moon , I was unsure what direction Luigi’s Mansion had taken since the original’s release 20 years ago. LM3 's star is the ritzy resort setting that gives way to stratified cultural hubs on each floor, a la Bioshock , ramping up the high-budget spectacle at the expense of the original’s tempered density.

Cooperative play is also wonderfully asymmetric, one player taking control of Luigi and the other the gelatinous Gooigi, but it never truly capitalizes on their ‘material’ differences. It’s caught halfway between an interesting multiplayer experiment and a frantic, body-swapping single-player experience, and falls just short of both.

March 2021, NS

Super Mario Maker 2 (2019) - ★★☆☆

dev. Nintendo EPD, dir. Yosuke Oshino

SMM2 is all about more - more modes, tools, objects, people. It’s a buffet of content, but few creators seem able to reign in the potential of the available tools, and their ideas all manifest in similar, community-certified ways.

Instead of real mechanical curiosity, the game revolves around a select few popular subgenres, most stemming from the masturbatory culture of Mario romhacks, a downward spiral to see who could create the hardest, cheapest, trickiest levels imaginable. Even Nintendo, imitating the masses in the new story mode, champions these ideas.

The tools are there, but nobody is really making Mario at all.

April 2021, NS

Sokobond (2013) - ★★★☆

dev. + dir. Alan Hazelden & Harry Lee

Marketed as an “elegantly designed puzzle game about chemistry,” Sokobond lives up to the billing. I enjoy the game’s title for its cheeky transparency: it is a series of clever wrinkles on the the classic sokoban block-pushing puzzler, but with a more interesting player goal of assembling, and thereby complicating, atomic structures that make traversal through the claustrophobic grids difficult.

Sokobond ’s cleverest quality is its clustered puzzle selection menu upon the Periodic Table, one that can be abstracted as a physical place - maybe a museum - with localized, iterating themes. Its presence turns the act of pushing circles into a journey.

April 2021, PC

Gunfire Reborn (2020) - ★★★☆

dev. Duoyi (Hong Kong) Interactive Entertainment

Gunfire Reborn is a looter-shooter and loves it. It boasts a primitive game loop of steadily scaling weapons, abilities, health bars, and multipliers, but tosses the fascination with player builds that last for hundreds of hours and keep the experience static.

It’s here that Duoyi find a compelling use of the roguelite format. Leveraging digestible play sessions (an hour max, a la Monster Hunter ) and an expanding roster of characters with multiple playstyles each, the player is given opportunities for rapid, iterative, real-time build prototyping, with the catch of RNG determining your available paths. It’s sleek, smart, fast-and-loose role playing.

May 2021, PC

A Good Snowman Is Hard to Build (2013) - ★★★☆

dev. + dir. Alan Hazelden & Benjamin Davis

A Good Snowman... successfully builds upon Sokobond 's sleek, compact design and pseudo-physical spaces. In particular, the recontextualization of the now terminally abstract block-pushing puzzler back into the physical work it once described is surprisingly weighty. Successfully building the snowmen unlocks more rooms in a quaint, labyrinthine puzzle garden, turning the extrinsic reward into something playful and natural.

A very small mechanical pool (namely, the snow on the ground that grows the snowballs) left me initially sore after Sokobond 's voracious experimentation, but the post-game dream world boldly asserts your originally valid solutions to be inadequate. It's a different, equally pleasurable audacity.

May 2021, PC
