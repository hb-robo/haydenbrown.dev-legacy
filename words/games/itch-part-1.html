Scratching the Itch, Part 1

  • November 30, 2020

  • 9 min read

In June 2020, an explosive month following the abhorrent and highly-televised murder of George Floyd by the Minneapolis Police Department, a group of over 1700 independent artists, game developers, writers, and programmers on Itch.io assembled a collection of their works with all proceeds split between the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund and the Community Bail Fund.

Beyond an opportunity to show financial support for an unambiguously good cause, for me this bundle offered a place to jump into the weird world of independent, hobbyist game design that Itch represented as a platform. In November that same year, I delved into the works from the bundle that caught my eye - these are the ones that I felt compelled to write about in my month of exploration.

I want to mention that although some of these writings veer into harsh-ish criticism, these developers and many others like them deserve your support for their hard work. Independent designers and developers, on the whole, push the envelope in ways that bigger companies simply can't - without them, the medium as a whole is nothing.

Sandcastles (2017) - ★★★✩

dev. & dir. Vectorpark (aka Patrick Smith)

Upon booting up the game with no prior knowledge of what it contained, Sandcastles ' concept and message are made immediately clear. You are free to build as many structures as you are able before the waves come by and they fall back into the ground... and the waves always come.

The shockingly brief lifespan of your creations is provocative. My first thoughts were about how many real structures had been eroded by nature in the time humanity has inhabited the earth, how even what feels fortressed and impenetrable is eventually lost to time. I was next met with how much I appreciated the small personal touches to my castles - the twists, the bends, the warps - and how their existence felt more meaningful after they were gone. I was even confronted with my feelings of inadequacy in my own works of art, and how ultimately unimportant the flaws in them actually are, as they too will be washed away to rubble in turn.

There's not much to the gameplay loop of Sandcastles , but it will leave a lasting impression on the player, as they reconsider their relationship with the impermanent.

Butterfly Soup (2017) - ★★★½

Pom Gets Wi-Fi (2013) - ★½✩✩

dev. & dir. Brianna Lei

It is difficult to find anything so easy to love as Butterfly Soup . A solo project by writer Brianna Lei, the game follows four young Asian-Americans through 2008, as they navigate their feelings, futures, and precarious relationships with their families. The cast of characters is multi-dimensional and feels fully-considered, with realistic stressors, desires, and worries. Yes, it is a visual novel, and yes, there is a level of commitment required of the player to stomach re-exposure to the most embarrassing bits of adolescence, but the thematic heft of the story is too powerful and joyous not to recommend.

The emotional thread is the thin line between confusion and power that comes from identifying queerness in youth, but there’s so much more layered throughout: the hidden, carefully plotted acts of love as rebellion; the embrace of athletics as a social outlet and marker of personal growth; the dissonance of a personhood and public-facing persona that don’t align; and finally, the comfort of becoming yourself along with your loved ones, imperfections be damned. Butterfly Soup is a fully-realized vertical slice of adolescence, and it's brilliant.

Lei’s first game, Pom Gets Wi-Fi , doesn’t hold the same charms. It’s a short parody-JRPG romp, wherein the titular Pom searches for a decent wi-fi connection in dog heaven. It’s filled to the brim with puns, backtracking, and 2013 pop culture, and fatally, the writing relies far too heavily on then-trendy internet humor that’s unsurprisingly aged like milk.

It occasionally shows an emotional side that would be greatly developed in Butterfly Soup (best of all being an observatory for the dogs to look down at their former owners on Earth), and the humor and length keep the experience fairly punchy, but the meme obsession ultimately overwhelms the experience. The concept is still novel and the presentation is charming, but at this point it’s best used as a time capsule of a simpler, more primitive era of internet culture.

Far From Noise (2017) - ★★½✩

dev. & dir. George Batchelor

Far From Noise is an unusual and refreshing narrative experience about a young woman stuck in a car teetering off the edge of a cliff. Unable to start the engine of the car because of its age, or to exit the vehicle without putting it in danger of falling, she hangs on the precipice of death, coming to terms with her life and the universe’s sense of humor.

The grip of the premise is initially blunted by flimsy, unconvincing writing, but I was pleasantly surprised to see it settle in over time, as various scenes improve the woman’s mental state and clarify her words. The otherworldly stag - neither confirmed to be a real animal or a figment of the protagonist’s rightfully active imagination - is a wonderfully stoic character, bringing moments of real serenity as he binds the situation and all its absurdity to the beauty of nature and the stars. The use of "quiet space" between lines of text was excellent and set a meditative pace for the experience.

You’re able to control some responses in conversation, but the hamhandedness of the choices keeps the player in the role of observer, absorbing the beauty and serenity second-hand from an arbitrary distance away. It invites you to share the moment, but never really makes an effort to bring you in. A deterministic structure might have been more affecting, but removing the only interactivity the game contains puts it closer to short film territory. Ultimately, Batchelor’s attempt at player-character synergy is a mixed bag, but even still, Far From Noise is a worthwhile exploration of absurdity, beauty, and peace.

Fortune-499 (2018) - ★★✩✩

dev. & dir. AP Thomson

Fortune-499 is a very compelling concept marred by some conflicting ideas of puzzle design and the core game mechanics. The game is about the manipulation of probability into fortuitous combat outcomes, but also about proving the value of one's work in a large corporation and the scant opportunity given to the miracle workers of the past as new management arrives.

The boring office setting is the stage of a demonic siege, and the literal probability wizard Cassie must travel to various floors to wipe out monsters like an IT admin completing service tickets. Cassie is witty and likeable, refusing to get into conflicts with monsters "off the clock" and teasing humanity's poor understanding of probability as a whole. Fights are riffs on rock-paper-scissors, using cards that influence the enemy's chances to choose one of rock, paper, or scissors to the player's benefit.

The idea of manipulating your enemy’s actions is clever, but the riffs on the mechanics become more grating and reset-heavy as as they require more precision. In one example, I had to intentionally lose a fight with an auditor boss to balance my win-loss ratio on the current floor and move on, but I was powerful enough to kill the boss in one hit while it needed to hit me upwards of 10 times before I died. In another, a three-pronged monster required me to precisely avoid death and bring each limb to 1 HP before finding a card that would prematurely end the fight. In both cases, randomness was the dividing line between success and failure.

The amount of deck experimentation required, the tedium of finding the solutions, and the pointless cost of failure are baffling decisions for a about influencing probability. Structuring battles around fixed desired outcomes (with punishment for failure) is a refusal from Thomson to play ball with his own concept of probability not reliable as a proxy for truth or certainty. Regardless, I'm looking forward to checking out the creator's other works as the major ideas, style, and pro-worker politics driving Fortune-499 are attention-grabbing.

Democratic Socialism Simulator (2020) - ★★½✩

dev. Molleindustria, dir. Paolo Pedercini

A soberingly exploration of the American socialist movement, Democratic Socialism Simulator offers a glimpse at the sheer immensity of the power structures witholding progress toward a better society in America by inviting you to the driver’s seat in the Oval Office. Cheekily, it doubles as an ego check for many players (including myself) who balk at the ineffectiveness of even the “good” public officials. What a horrific job!

With enemies in both parties and the press, and the everpresent tick of the clock, you will find not every item on the moral platform can be addressed without the risk of a coup, assassination, impeachment, or losing your job, and those events happen much more quickly than you might expect. You may also be confronted by cabinet members (and public figures such as the union leader) who take issue with your approach or your priorities, and the feedback they provide helped check me on more than a few of my poor or uninformed decisions.

My gripes with the experience are all mechanical, namely that assumptions that can be made if you have some awareness of government institutions can lead to unanticipated mistakes. Most egregiously, cutting big chunks out of the military budget would free up hundreds of billions of dollars annually in real life, but in-game it took the form of a not-so-large, single-time payment. This blunts the realism of the situation and can take theoretically viable governing strategies off of the table, but I also acknowledge that the game is a thought experiment first and foremost, and it doesn’t take away from the messaging as a whole.

Despite the game’s difficulty, I don't believe Molleindustria's Paolo Pedercini argues for an incrementalist approach, far from it. The lesson is in the enormity of the task that is liberation from within, that American politics is fraught with enemies of the working class, that the system itself is designed to eject those who dare to side with the masses. This is an educational game about shedding enthusiasm for reformation politics, and its honesty should be celebrated.

Minit (2018) - ★★✩✩

dev. & dir. Jan Willem Nijman, Kitty Calis, Jukio Kallio, & Dominik Johann

Minit is a series of 1-minute escapades into a Legend of Zelda-style world. Your objective is to venture out to explore and make incremental progress in quests before you die and wake up back at home with only your inventory preserved, a la Majora’s Mask . The pace is snappy and lighthearted, the game ushering you around its map and teasing areas you’ll have to remember to visit on your future runs.

The actual happenings of the world are secondary to the cutesy characters and striking 1-bit presentation, so its short length caught me by surprise. Our hero is cursed by the sword they find early in the game, trapping them in their Groundhog Day time-loop, but since the character’s motivations are kept hidden and the player’s familiarity with the world is so limited, what to do, how to do it, and what you’re even trying to accomplish in the longterm is obscured.

Replacing it is the moment-to-moment decision making and execution of miniature quests, most all of which will push up very close to your 60-second timer of existence. There’s always something to do, so much so that I ended up focusing on micro-task after micro-task until suddenly the credits started rolling. Without a marker of overall progress, it’s easy to feel like a much larger experience is being signaled to the player.

Worst of all is that the timer doesn’t lend itself to any challenges that require thinking outside of the linear-time box, and in fact outside of a small quest to repeatedly water a plant at the beginning of the game, repeated attempts at something are not required for progression. The result of this is a time mechanic that encourages hurried play, but not thoughtful play. Some of the puzzles are frantic and fun, and the writing has a dry wit that I enjoyed, but when everything moves at the pace demanded of you in Minit, it's hard to appreciate much of anything.

LYNE (2013) - ★½✩✩

dev. & dir. Thomas Bowker

LYNE is a puzzle game wherein you trace lines through various sets of shapes, minding the number of times you pass through shared hub slots that accept any line color but in limited quantities. This is a simple concept in theory but the puzzles can get quite tricky without ever expanding in actual size - everything is kept to a tidy 4x3 grid, at least as far as I've gotten. The interface is as intuitive as it gets for mobile platforms, the game itself very similar to unlocking an Android phone.

My major greivance is that while it's a nice brain teaser, I don't ever feel like I'm taking lessons away from the act of solving a puzzle. There are surely "tricks of the trade" that are meant to be learned, but with such an abstract interface and concept they have been lost on me. On many occasions the first combination of lines I entered happened to be the correct answer, so I'm not sure how much explicit planning is required when I can gut-feel my way through the game. This flaw make it a merely passable puzzler - without a consistent set of twists, I'm left perpetually unsurprised.

A Normal Lost Phone (2017) - ★★★½

dev. Accidental Queens, dir. Elizabeth Maler & Rafael Martinez-Jausoro

Directors Elizabeth Maler and Rafael Martinez-Jausoro have assembled one of the cleverest game concepts I have seen in some time. A Normal Lost Phone takes place on an Android-esque smartphone, with an obvious hook: you want to find the phone’s owner, Sam, and return their phone. To this end, the player must rifle through messages, emails, photos, and forum posts, sometimes with intimate and private details never meant for the public eye.

The game’s ambiguous goals give a relaxed pace, but spending time in a realistic world that isn't yours can feel invasive. I was captivated by the mystery surrounding Sam, and scoured for details to help me solve it, but in doing so I was confronted by my own willingness to cross boundaries, especially ones in place to protect people from harm. It’s powerful and sobering, and could’ve easily felt less impactful if the interface was less lifelike.

The game’s interface also creates powerful moments of diegesis. The soundtrack - a smattering of delicate, quaint indie pop - felt out of place to me until I realized it was actually playing from the in-game Music app. Such a simple and obvious choice, but surprising in its novelty. Using each app to find their related information feels just like normal daily life, whether it’s to zoom in on pictures to identify people or rummaging through old conversations for information. Not everything is intuitive; some apps have passwords that must be discovered, but they’re not just exclusively numeric, they’re by and large cryptic or irrelevant to the story. Progression isn't tied to your learned knowledge of Sam's life, but to dates and zip codes that are tangentially related. It stands out as a particularly game-y thing in a game that hardly feels like one.

It has its problems, but A Normal Lost Phone is tasteful, contemporary, and tells its own story with wit, confidence, warmth and pacing. What it should be remembered for, though, is the unspoken epiphanies of its storytelling vehicle - the inherent intrusiveness of altruism, opportunistic indulgence in the secret lives of others, vicarious projection of our social lives via the digital portals at our fingertips, the divide between private and public appearance and identity. This is what modernity in storytelling looks like.
