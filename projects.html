Missing Data Imputation in R: a research project

In the Spring 2020 semester, I wrote a data science pseudo-research paper as a capstone project for my final college course: Statistical Bioinformatics. My partner and I evaluated the efficacy of missing value imputation methods in R, using family-associated genomic data.

The project involved a thoroughly recorded exploration of family-wise data from snpStats, complete with visualization of missing values. The cleaned data was then put through an R pipeline that randomly removed a partition of values from the set. Using this "poked" set, a variety of freely available data imputation R packages were evaluated for their accuracy.

Detailed explanations of of the data exploration, cleaning, and pipeline were compiled in a research paper-styled report in LaTeX.

Languages & Tools: R (incl. ggplot2, mice, tidyverse), LaTeX

The project code and paper can be found on GitHub here .

Twitter News Credibility: a data analysis project

In the Fall 2019 semester, I was a part of a two-person data analysis project spanning the entire semester of a course in Data Science Applications in Social Media. It was almost completely independent and involved multiple steps of data curation, cleaning, and analysis, culminating with a mock research paper of our findings.

The pipeline used the Twitter 1% Stream API to scrape news articles from around 67 GB of tweet JSON files. Using a list of "valid" news sources per a panel of journalists, the links were evaluated for both their validty and bias, with additional analysis for the sources shared for specific trending political topics.

A Reddit component of the project was planned and developed but was ultimately dropped before the project was completed.

Languages & Tools: Python, mongoDB, tmux, LaTeX

The project's code can be found hosted on GitHub here .

Experimental Plus-Minus: a basketball statistic

In the spring of 2019, I made a pretty flawed NBA impact aggregate stat: Experimental Plus-Minus (EXPM). The project was an attempt to arrive at player value from the opposite direction of modern plus-minus stats. It instead identifies individual skills in players and builds them up into a holistic evaluation.

EXPM is quite flawed. For one, trying to pigeonhole the ways players can create value is fairly unintuitive. It also has no defensive component, as it was more difficult to find good data for. Interestingly, the LEBRON stat from BBall Index , despite its awful name, takes a similar approach to EXPM, but with actual statistical rigor and no missing value areas.

Languages & Tools: Google Sheets, data from NBA.com and Basketball Reference

To play around with the findings (2018-19 only) or check out the original EXPM manifesto, check out the full write-up here .

VirLab: an undergrad genomics research team

From spring 2018 until summer 2019, I was a part of an undergraduate research team for the Binghamton computer science department. Our goal was to use patterns of k-mers (variably lengthed substrings of RNA strands) to identify strands of the Flaviviridae virus family in animal waste.

Our pipeline parsed FASTA files into string objects, collected all k-mer counts of a given k range, filtered out statistically insignificant k-mers using a Kruskal-Wallis algorithm, and classified viral strands with an SVM. We met weekly for pair programming sessions, and presented our findings with virologists from The Cary Institute and Arizona State University.

VirLab was my very first data science project and I appreciate it greatly for that alone, even if it never ended with publication.

Languages & Tools: Python (incl. NumPy, SciKit), BBMap

The project's code can be found hosted on GitHub here .

FakeNewsDetector: a 1st-place Hackathon project

In February 2018, a few of my close friends and I entered our first 24-hour Hackathon, Binghamton University's HackBU. Our final submitted project, a neural network that could evaluate any news link for its "validity" using a variety of feature proxies, would go on to take the first overall prize.

All of us were inexperienced at the time, and as such we spent most of the night pouring over TensorFlow documentation. The final neural net evaluates articles based on their reading level, host geolocation, the age of the hosting source, its similarity to other articles (as many fake news sites upload in batches), and a variety of word processing tools including a typo scraper.

The neural net was trained on catalogued instances of fake and "real" news (the latter including satire sources like The Onion), and it was able to evaluate news validity with a >95% success rate.

Languages & Tools: Python (incl. TensorFlow, Flask)

The project's code can be found hosted on GitHub here .
